## 🔭 The Big Question This Week

### When AI Writes 80% of Its Own Code—How Close Is "Recursive Self-Improvement"?

The most mind-bending number this week isn't Anthropic's $965B valuation or ChatGPT's 1 billion MAU—it's this: **as of May 2026, over 80% of the code merged into Anthropic's codebase was written by Claude.**

Let that sink in.

Before Claude Code launched in early 2025, that number was in the single digits. In barely over a year, AI went from "occasionally autocompleting a few lines" to "authoring the vast majority of a $47B-revenue company's code." Engineers are merging 8x more code daily than in 2024, and quality is climbing too—Claude's success rate on the hardest open-ended tasks hit 76% in May, up 50% in six months.

**What does this mean?**

There's a concept in the history of science called "recursive self-improvement" (RSI)—when a system can improve itself, and the improved version can improve itself even better, creating a positive feedback loop. Anthropic's warning is precisely this: AI is approaching that threshold.

But the reactions are fascinating.

Anthropic says "everyone should slow down," yet they just raised $65B and filed for an IPO—that's not braking, that's flooring it while yelling "be careful!" Critics call it "regulatory capture 101": use safety narratives to constrain competitors (especially open-source models) while flexing your own capabilities. Former Wall Street analyst Tae Kim put it bluntly: "Fear-based marketing designed to front-run regulation ahead of their IPO."

But here's the thing—**look at the data, not the motives.**

80% is an irreversible signal. It means AI writing AI isn't theoretical anymore; it's the daily reality at a company with $47B in annual revenue. If you extrapolate—Anthropic says the doubling period for tasks AI can complete autonomously has shrunk from 7 months to 4 months—then AI systems autonomously iterating on themselves could arrive well before the end of 2027.

**What does this mean for us?**

For the games and design industry, the immediate impact of recursive self-improvement isn't AI replacing designers (not yet). It's that **the tool chain will iterate faster than humans can learn it.**

Consider: this week alone, Microsoft dropped 7 self-developed MAI models; OpenAI merged Codex into ChatGPT for 1 billion users; 4 companies launched AI design agents in 5 weeks. Tools update monthly, but workflow adaptation happens quarterly. When AI update speed > human adaptation speed, there's a growing "capability time lag."

**Those who learn to collaborate with AI first will hold the advantage**—not because AI replaces you, but because those who don't use AI will increasingly struggle to match the output of those who do.

So the real question isn't "will AI slow down?"—it won't. It's: **when AI starts building itself faster than we can understand it, how do we redefine our own value as users?**

Anthropic co-founder Jack Clark said it well: "The role of humans in AI development will significantly diminish, and our focus will likely shift to supervision, verification, and review."

In design-industry terms: **the future designer isn't "the person who draws"—it's "the person who judges whether the AI drew it right."**

That's not a demotion. It's an upgrade. Judgment is scarcer than execution, and far harder to automate.

An open question to sit with: If AI writes 80% of the code and maintains quality, what exactly is the 20% that humans bring to design when AI handles 80% of the first drafts?
