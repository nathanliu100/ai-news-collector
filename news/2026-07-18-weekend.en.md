## 🔭 One Big Question This Week

### AI Video Prices Crashed 80% Overnight — But Does Cheaper Actually Make Better Content?

This week's AI video market can be summed up in one word: insane.

July 9, 10 PM: Kuaishou launched Kling 4 in San Francisco — 60s 4K, multi-shot, native dialogue, priced at ~$0.03/second. Less than 12 hours later, Google DeepMind slashed Veo 4 from $0.50 to $0.10/second. Five hours after that, OpenAI cut Sora 3 API pricing by 60%, from $0.15 to $0.06/second.

In a single day, the cost of generating one second of 4K video collapsed from fifty cents to three cents. The industry is calling it "Year One of the AI Video Price War."

Don't celebrate just yet. Cheaper is good. Cheaper does not mean better content.

There's a classic cognitive trap here. When the cost of a tool approaches zero, the axis of competition shifts — from "who can use the tool" to "who came prepared before the tool existed."

#### The Digital Camera Lesson

Think of this week's AI video price war as the digital camera revolution of the early 2000s. Photography went from "buy film → develop → select" to "press shutter → check screen → delete and retry." Cost hit zero. Everyone said this would be the golden age of photography.

What happened? Photo volume exploded. Good photos did not.

Hundreds of millions of photos are uploaded to Instagram daily. But the ones that make you stop scrolling are still taken by people with compositional instinct, narrative sense, and an eye for the "decisive moment." The difference isn't the camera — everyone uses an iPhone. The difference is what that person was thinking before they pressed the shutter.

AI video follows the same logic.

#### What Cheap Actually Unlocks

The good news first. 4K video crashing from $0.50/s to $0.03/s directly benefits experimentation.

Before, making a 30-second promo video meant generating 2-3 versions at most — each one cost real money. Now, the same budget gets you 30-50 variants. A/B testing sample sizes go from "barely enough" to "statistically significant."

For IEG creative teams, this means batch-producing the same concept in different styles, color schemes, and copy variants — doing in a day what used to take a week. Seedance 2.5's 50 multimodal reference inputs + 3D white-box preview ensure consistency across batch output.

But here comes the subtle part.

#### What Cheap Can't Fix

When 4K video costs pennies per second, everyone can mass-produce — including your competitors. At that point, three things separate the winners:

**One: Aesthetic judgment.** Which 3 of those 50 variants are worth running? AI can tell you which video has the highest completion rate, but it can't tell you which video makes people want to watch it again. That judgment comes from your intuition about your audience, your understanding of platform dynamics, your taste in "what's good." Taste cannot be automated.

**Two: Narrative ability.** 30 seconds of native 4K is impressive. But telling a memorable story in those 30 seconds isn't about resolution. Tim (from Yingshi Hurricane) made the point well in a daily briefing comment: Seedance's 30 seconds is a complete short video length, but "if you can only accept what it gives you without precise control over camera movement, lighting, or facial expressions — it's still a demo tool, not a production tool." Narrative control is something AI can't yet hand you.

**Three: Differentiated positioning.** When everyone's videos "look good," what makes yours memorable? The answer is not "I used the latest model" — that information is invisible to the viewer. The answer lies in your understanding of brand tone, your consistent command of a game's world-building, your precise feel for what resonates with your target players.

#### This Isn't an AI Problem. It's a People Problem.

Interestingly, that 17-year-old's "fake AI" website this week provides the perfect counter-proof. 25 million people, knowing full well there's a human on the other end, still chose to play "pretend it's AI" on his site. Why? Because in an era of AI content overload, **humanity has become the scarce resource**.

When AI video is so cheap you can generate without thinking, content that shows intention, personal style, and "human touch" will paradoxically stand out more. Just like how a grainy film photo draws more attention in a feed full of perfectly retouched images.

#### So What?

"Cheap" isn't permission to mass-produce brainlessly. It's permission to redirect the money and energy you've saved into the things that actually matter. Time saved → invest in creative refinement. Money saved → invest in smarter targeting tests. Mental bandwidth saved → invest in building a distinctive brand visual system.

When generation is no longer the bottleneck, taste becomes the moat.
