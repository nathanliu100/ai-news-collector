## 🔭 This Week's Big Question

### OpenAI's Growth Stalled — Is "General AI" a Flawed Business Model?

This week's most underrated news wasn't the trial — it was WSJ's exclusive: **OpenAI missed both revenue and user growth internal targets.**

The numbers:
- OpenAI has locked in ~$6,000 billion in future compute spending (Oracle $3,000B + Amazon $1,000B + Microsoft $130B+)
- Anthropic is stealing market share in coding and enterprise
- CFO Sarah Friar expressed concerns about funding sustainability
- That same week, DeepSeek V4 offered nearly identical capabilities at **1/9th the price** of GPT-5.5

This isn't just an OpenAI problem. It's a deeper structural issue: **"AI that can do everything" is hard to monetize.**

Think about it — why did you last open ChatGPT? Probably to "look something up" or "help edit some text." Use it and leave, fundamentally no different from a search engine. But OpenAI's valuation is built on the assumption that users will pay $200/month for the Pro tier.

Meanwhile, why is Anthropic gaining share? Because **Claude Code is embedded directly in developers' terminals** — it's not a "do anything" chatbox, but a high-efficiency tool for a specific workflow. This is what we might call "knowledge granularity": not selling you an encyclopedia, but giving you the exact line you need in the exact second you need it.

Here's the counterintuitive insight: **The more general the AI, the weaker the business model.** Because general means:
1. No irreplaceability (DeepSeek does the same thing at 1/9 the price)
2. No workflow lock-in (users can switch to competitors anytime)
3. No data moat (your chat history doesn't create a barrier)

Look at this week's evidence chain: Microsoft gives up OpenAI exclusivity (decides it's not worth locking in) → WSJ reports missed growth → DeepSeek valuation surges from $10B to $30B → TIME ranks ByteDance #1 (for distribution power, not model quality).

Conclusion? **AI competition's endgame isn't at the model layer — it's at the application and distribution layer.** Whoever embeds AI into users' existing workflows (WeChat, TikTok, VS Code, terminal) wins. The model itself is becoming "utilities" — essential but not premium-priced.

What does this mean for game multimedia design teams? Stop agonizing over "which model to use" — the API pricing war will only intensify. The real question is: **Can you embed AI into the design pipeline you already use every day**, making it your "obedient worker" (Tencent Hunyuan Hy3's positioning), rather than an external tool that requires switching windows to "ask questions"?
