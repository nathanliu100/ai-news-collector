## 🔭 The Big Question This Week

### When Your Competitor Pays You $1.25 Billion Monthly Rent — The "Infrastructure Paradox" of the AI Era

This week, SpaceX's S-1 filing revealed a jaw-dropping number: Anthropic pays **$1.25 billion per month** in compute fees to SpaceX's Colossus supercomputer center. Contract runs through 2029, totaling ~$45 billion.

Let's translate this scenario: Musk dissolved his AI company xAI last year and leased 220,000 GPUs to Anthropic — his former competitor. Now Anthropic uses those GPUs to train Claude, beating OpenAI (Musk's other former company). Musk collects rent.

Sounds like a sci-fi plot? But it reveals a profound structural truth about the AI era: **controlling compute infrastructure matters more than owning models.**

In traditional tech competition, you either build the platform or the app — platform builders don't sell shovels to competitors. But AI-era logic is different:

1. **Compute is a physical asset** with operational costs. 220,000 idle GPUs just burn electricity. Better to let competitors pay your power bill.
2. **Models are commoditizing.** This week's data: Chinese AI models now account for 60%+ of OpenRouter traffic, with Tencent Hunyuan ranked #1 globally. When models become "good enough," pricing power evaporates.
3. **Infrastructure has economies of scale.** Once you've built a 1GW data center and deployed Starlink for connectivity, marginal costs decline. Model training is the opposite — it gets more expensive the more you train.

This creates an "infrastructure paradox": stronger models need more compute; more expensive compute makes model companies more dependent on infrastructure providers. The biggest profits don't go to the smartest model, but to the shovel sellers.

NVIDIA's Q1 revenue hit $81.6B with $58.3B net profit — one quarter's profit exceeds most tech companies' annual revenue. The stock still dropped 3% after hours (market felt it wasn't "exceeding expectations enough"). Anthropic may have achieved its first quarterly profit ($559M in Q2), but the $1.25B monthly compute bill eats most of that margin.

What does this mean for our games/design industry?

When AI video tools (Seedance, Runway, Kling) all tell the story of "99% cost reduction," what's truly worth watching isn't whether generating a video is cheap — it's **who controls the compute needed to generate that video**. Seedance 2.0 proved at Cannes that 15 people can make a 95-minute film in 14 days for under $500K. But how much compute infrastructure did ByteDance invest? Volcano Engine's GPU clusters are the real moat.

So next time someone asks "will AI let everyone make movies?" — the correct answer is: **Technically yes, but economically no.** Imagination is free. Compute never is.
