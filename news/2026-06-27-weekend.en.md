## 🔭 One Big Question This Week

### AI Revenue Just Crossed Depreciation Costs — Is the Trillion-Dollar Bet Actually Paying Off?

A number flew under most people's radar this week, but it might be the most important inflection signal of 2026 for the AI industry:

**Global generative AI annualized revenue hit $175 billion, with quarterly revenue exceeding infrastructure depreciation costs for the first time.**

What does this mean? Simply put: the AI industry is finally starting to "feed itself."

For two years, Wall Street's existential question has been: are all these companies burning cash on data centers with real paying customers, or is this a glorified Ponzi scheme? As of this week, the answer is shifting to "yes, there are real customers."

But hold the champagne.

**The counterintuitive part: revenue covering depreciation ≠ investment payback.**

Here's an imperfect but vivid analogy: you spent $1 million opening a bubble tea shop, depreciating at $30K/month. Revenue finally exceeds $30K — but what about your $1M principal? At this rate, payback takes 30 years.

The AI industry faces the same math, just with scarier numbers:
- OpenAI Q1 revenue $5.7B, losses $9.3B — burning more than earning
- Global AI data center capex projected to exceed $500B in 2026
- AI stocks dipped three straight days this week on ROI anxiety

So why is everyone still pouring money in?

Because AI economics has a unique "electricity moment" happening: **token prices are plummeting, but consumption is rising even faster.**

DeepSeek V4.1 cut per-token costs 15% vs. last gen. Doubao's pricing is a race to the floor. Logic says price cuts should compress revenue — but the opposite is happening: cheaper means 10x more users, and the total pie grows larger.

This mirrors early electrification perfectly: electricity prices dropped from $1/kWh to $0.01/kWh, but global consumption grew 10,000x, and power companies became richer than ever.

**For those of us in design and creative work, the implication is clear:**

AI tool costs will keep falling. Today, making a 30-second video with Seedance 2.5 might cost tens of dollars in API fees. A year from now, maybe just a few cents. When costs drop to "use it casually" levels — the way we create will fundamentally change. Not "I have a great idea worth spending money on," but "let me try 100 ideas and see what clicks."

From "carefully preparing one creation" to "low-cost mass experimentation" — this is the creative paradigm shift that every cost collapse in history has triggered since the Industrial Revolution.

**An open question to sit with:**

When AI inference marginal cost approaches zero, what becomes of "human thinking"? If AI can try 1,000 design options for free, your value is "picking the best one" — but what if AI can pick too? In the end, the only irreplaceable human capability might be "deciding what you want" itself.
