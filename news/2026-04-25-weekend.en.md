## 🔭 The Big Question This Week

### AI models ship every two months now — is speed always a good thing?

GPT-5.4 to GPT-5.5: less than two months. Kimi K2.6 emerged out of nowhere as the first open-source model to beat all closed-source models on mainstream coding benchmarks. Qwen3.6-Max-Preview followed within days. DeepSeek V4 is about to drop.

April 2026 is the most intense model release month in AI history — but do we actually need this pace?

Let's start with the numbers. GPT-5.5 scored 82.7% on Terminal-Bench coding — state of the art. Impressive. But just a week after Claude Opus 4.7 launched, users started complaining it felt "dumber" in everyday conversations — benchmarks up, user experience down. If science writer Wan Weigang saw this, he'd probably say: **"When we can't measure what truly matters, we start chasing what's easy to measure."**

That's exactly what's happening across the AI industry.

Models get faster, but productization can't keep up. While OpenAI is busy releasing models, it's also shutting down Sora (dying tomorrow), disbanding its science team, and watching three executives walk out the same day. Anthropic launched Opus 4.7 while quietly hiking prices from $20 to $100. Model releases have become an arms race — not because users need better models, but because competitors released new ones.

In biology, there's a concept called the **Red Queen Effect**: in a fiercely competitive ecosystem, every species must keep running just to stay in place. Lewis Carroll wrote this scene in *Through the Looking-Glass* — the Red Queen tells Alice: "Now, here, you see, it takes all the running you can do, to keep in the same place."

The AI industry is living this right now.

But here's what's interesting: Moonshot AI's Kimi K2.6 opened a different door — **open-source beat closed-source at the top for the first time in mainstream coding benchmarks.** What does this mean? It means "running fast" is no longer a big-company monopoly. When open-source models can match the best, models themselves stop being moats.

The real question becomes: who can turn a model into **a product users genuinely can't live without?**

SpaceX locked in Cursor for $60 billion not because Cursor has the best model, but because 3 million developers use it every day. Canva AI 2.0's "persistent memory" retains users better than any benchmark score. Google welded AI into Gmail/Docs/Sheets — 3 billion users became AI users overnight, no new model launch needed.

For game multimedia design teams, this insight is practical: don't chase every new model release. **Pick a tool that's good enough and deeply embed it into your workflow.** GPT Image 2's Chinese rendering is already solid — get your promo material pipeline running. Claude Design's structured design output works for prototypes. A tool's value isn't about being "the strongest" — it's about being "the smoothest."

In a world where models ship every two months, the only winners are people who **don't care about model version numbers, only about whether today's work got done.**
